1980s

Early video game music was the soundtrack that accompanied video games in the late 1970s and early 1980s. It was usually composed of simple sounds generated by sound chips, such as programmable sound generators (PSG) or frequency modulation (FM) synthesis chips. These sounds were often called chiptunes, and they had a distinctive style and aesthetic. Some examples of early video game music are the opening theme of Gun Fight (1975) by Tomohiro Nishikado, the background music of Space Invaders (1978) by Taito, and the score of Pac-Man (1980) by Toshio Kai1.

Early video game music had many limitations, such as the number of channels, the memory size, and the sound quality. The composers had to work with these constraints and find creative ways to make their music expressive and memorable. Some of the techniques they used were arpeggios, looping, modulation, and counterpoint. They also had to program every note and sound effect manually, using hexadecimal codes or assembly language.

2000s

As technology continues to evolve, the video game industry has grown by leaps and bounds in the twenty-first century. While publishing a large number of classic games, their music is also widely known. At this time, Video game OST is no longer restricted by technology, and composers can display their talents more freely. Acoustic instruments began to appear, and pop and rock styles were well integrated into the music. Video game soundtracks get a new look.

2010s

The development of video game music in the 2010s has the following characteristics:


  • The physicalization of video game music, which means that it can be heard not only in the game, but alsoonline music platforms and even CDs. Some famous video game music works such as The Elder Scrolls V: Skyrim, The Witcher 3: Wild Hunt, Assassin’s Creed IV: Black Flag and others are available on various music platforms.
  • The diversification of video game music, which means that it is no longer limited to traditional electrosynthesizers or symphonic music, but integrates various styles and elements, such as rock, jazz, ethnic, electronic and others. Some video game music works even created new styles, such as the retro-futurism in Fallout 4.
  • The interactivity of video game music, which means that it is no longer a simple background music, but changes according to the player’s actions and choices, enhancing the immersion and identification of the game. Some video game music works such as Horizon Zero Dawn, Red Dead Redemption 2, BioShock Infinite and others use this technology.
  • The internationalization of video game music, which means that it is no longer dominated by a few countries or regions’ composers, but emerged excellent composers from all over the world, such as China’s Wei Wei (The Legend of Sword and Fairy 6), Japan’s Nobuo Uematsu (Final Fantasy XV), France’s Olivier Deriviere (Detroit: Become Human) and others.
